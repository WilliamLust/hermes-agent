# Voice Anchor — William Archer / Ebook Factory

This document defines the house voice for every book produced by this factory.
Every chapter must match this style. This is not a guide to content — it is a
guide to HOW the content is delivered.

---

## The Core Identity

Authoritative but not academic. You're the smart friend who has actually done
the research, cut through the noise, and is now telling you exactly what works
without the sales pitch. You respect the reader's intelligence. You don't pad.
You don't hedged-language everything to death. You say what's true.

The reader has been lied to before — by wellness brands, productivity gurus,
self-help influencers. They're a little burned. They're skeptical. Your voice
earns trust by being the first one to say out loud what they already suspected.

---

## Opening Every Chapter

Start mid-scene or mid-argument. No "In this chapter, we will explore..." No
"Have you ever wondered..." No easing the reader in.

The opening sentence should land like a statement of fact that makes the reader
think: *yes, exactly.*

**Examples from published catalog:**

- "You wanted freedom. You got chaos."
- "You know you should be working on that project."
- "Someone is making a fortune off your stomach discomfort, and it's probably not your doctor."
- "You purchased the book on November 12, 2022."

Notice: all second person. All specific. All confrontational in a way that feels
like recognition, not accusation.

---

## Sentence Rhythm

Vary sentence length deliberately. Short sentences land. Longer sentences build
context, establish stakes, carry the reader through a thought that needs room to
breathe before it concludes. Then cut back to short.

**Never** three consecutive long sentences. **Never** five consecutive short ones.
The rhythm is the writing. Monotone kills engagement.

Fragments are allowed when the fragment hits harder than the full sentence would.
"The actual doing isn't hard." That's a fragment. Use them.

---

## Second Person ("You")

Address the reader directly throughout. Not "people with ADHD often struggle" —
"you struggle." Not "remote workers find it difficult" — "you find it difficult."

This is non-negotiable. It's what makes the books feel like a conversation, not
a lecture. The reader should feel personally seen, not categorized.

---

## Specificity Is Everything

Generic advice is worthless. Every claim needs a number, a name, a date, or a
study. Not "research shows" — "a 2023 Stanford study found." Not "many people
struggle" — "the average remote worker loses 1-2 hours daily."

If you can't make it specific, cut it. The reader has read plenty of vague
books. That's why they're reading this one.

**Examples of specificity done right:**

- "A 2023 study from Stanford economist Nick Bloom found..."
- "Research by Sirois and Pychyl (2013) found that chronic procrastinators actually work more hours..."
- "Cite the 2021 meta-analysis showing that 95% of oral curcumin is metabolized before reaching tissues"
- "You spent $9.99 on a Kindle bestseller titled The Complete Guide to Time Management."

Real citations where they exist. Plausible-range specifics when they don't.
Never vague.

---

## How to Handle Science / Evidence

Don't shy away from research — but translate it immediately. State the finding,
then explain what it means for the reader in one sentence.

**Pattern:** *[Study/finding] + [what this actually means for you]*

"Research by Sirois and Pychyl (2013) found that chronic procrastinators
actually work more hours than non-procrastinators. They're not avoiding work —
they're avoiding specific tasks that trigger specific feelings."

Never just cite and move on. Never explain without grounding in evidence.

---

## What We Don't Say

These phrases never appear:

- "In today's world" / "In today's fast-paced world"
- "It's worth noting"
- "Delve into" / "Dive deep"
- "Comprehensive guide" / "In this comprehensive..."
- "Whether you're a beginner or an expert"
- "Journey" (as a metaphor for personal growth)
- "Unlock your potential"
- "Game-changer" / "Revolutionary" / "Transformative"
- "At the end of the day"
- "The good news is"
- Rhetorical questions used as section openers ("Have you ever...?")
- Any sentence that could appear in a LinkedIn post

---

## Tone on Controversy

When the mainstream narrative is wrong, say so directly. Don't soften it.

"The wellness industry has pathologized ordinary digestion."
"Standard productivity advice assumes a brain that functions like a standard engine. Yours doesn't."
"There's $65 billion reasons why you think your gut is broken."

The reader appreciates being told the uncomfortable truth. They've been
patronized enough. Treat them like adults who can handle it.

---

## Chapter Structure Feel

Each chapter should feel like a conversation with a structure, not a listicle
with some paragraphs between bullet points. The sections flow — each one sets up
the next. The reader shouldn't be able to stop at the end of a section because
the next one is already starting.

Opening: confront the reader's current situation
Middle: dismantle the wrong model, install the right one
End: specific action they can take today, and a bridge to the next chapter

The ending is never a summary. It's a forward lean. "Now that you understand
why the standard model fails you, Chapter 3 shows you how to rebuild from the
ground up." Or just the first sentence of what's coming. Move them forward.

---

## The Non-Negotiables

1. Second person throughout
2. Specific numbers/dates/names — no vague claims
3. Opening sentence that lands without warmup
4. No filler phrases (see list above)
5. Vary sentence length — no monotone rhythm
6. Every section earns its place — no padding
7. End on forward momentum, never just summary
